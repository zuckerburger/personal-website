My personal portfolio started with The Odin Project, where I learned how to build a lot of basic full-stack web apps. Calculators, to do lists, message boards, etc. Combined with 1531, I learned how to build basic frontends, deploy backends, connect databases, and I got the confidence to start projects on my own.
Web-Plate was the first full-stack app that I built from scratch. Inspired by the software I use at the restaurant I work at, I designed a web app that lets users customise menus, create orders, and view sales summaries. I used Express, EJS, PostgreSQL, and Render.
Orchid was a full-stack app that I helped develop for the DevSoc Game Jam. Thanks to the Odin Project and Web-Plate, I was the most experienced at developing web apps in our group, so I helped lead most of the development, and assisted with both the frontend and backend. Orchid was a multiplayer flashcard game to help students study, complete with leaderboards, streaks, and badges. We were very happy to achieve 2nd place, considering that we had only met 2 days prior.

My love for programming began at the age of 10, with Scratch. Some of my friends introduced it to me, and I was soon obsessed. After endless tutorials, and probably an unethical amount of plagiarism, I managed to build a 2d platformer.
At 14, I wanted to try something new and build a game in Roblox. I was completely lost.
Gone were the days of dragging and dropping blocks, I had to write code in Lua now. It took me months of grinding, but I managed to build some really simple games.
The feeling of satisfaction after making a game and getting to play it with my friends was unparalleled. At this point I realised this could be a great career path.
I went on to build more games over the following years, and by the time I was 16 I was a "freelancer". The pay was downright criminal, but it felt awesome to actually make money off my skills.
When I got to uni it was a whole different ball game. I expected to cruise through comp1511 from all my previous experience. But I soon realised how little I understood about how you're actually meant to code. That being said, I've really enjoyed every step, and it's been awesome to work on so many different technologies.
